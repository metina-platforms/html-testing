Class Assignment Prompt 3: Propose ≥3 governance actions (Purpose, Design, Assumptions, Risks).
Answer (3 sample actions)
Action 1 — “Audit-first lab assistant” (technical strategy)
  • Purpose: reduce preventable errors in dilution/pipetting & improve documentation.
  • Design: logged recommendations; citations to protocol steps; mandatory user confirmation on risky steps.
  • Assumptions: protocols are available; users will follow prompts; models are reasonably calibrated.
  • Risks: overreliance; false confidence; “paperwork” burden if UX is bad.
Action 2 — “Role-based access + red-teaming” (org/ops)
  • Purpose: prevent dual-use misuse; constrain features by user role (student/TA/PI).
  • Design: permissions; safe-mode defaults; periodic red-team tests; incident reporting process.
  • Assumptions: identity & role can be verified; threat models are maintained.
  • Risks: lockout of legitimate users; “security theater” if tests aren’t serious.
Action 3 — “Standards-aligned safety checklist” (policy/requirement)
  • Purpose: align training & workflows with biosafety / waste handling norms.
  • Design: required checklists for certain workflows; exportable compliance reports.
  • Assumptions: checklists map to real risk; labs accept lightweight compliance tooling.
  • Risks: box-ticking; uneven adoption; may impede research if too rigid.

J1
Professor Jacobson Q1: What is the error rate of polymerase? How does it compare to the length of the human genome, and how does biology deal with the discrepancy?
Answer
Replicative polymerases have intrinsic error rates that are greatly reduced by multiple layers of correction. With proofreading and mismatch repair, the final mutation rate can be on the order of ~10-10 per base per replication (ballpark; varies by organism, context, and assay).

Comparison to genome length: The human genome is ~3.2×109 bp. If errors were ~10-10/bp, you’d expect ~0.3 errors per replication on average. Even if the realized rate is somewhat higher in practice, biology still keeps it to “a few or fewer” errors per cell division rather than millions.

How biology handles it: (1) base selection in the polymerase active site, (2) proofreading exonuclease activity, (3) mismatch repair, plus (4) checkpoints, apoptosis, and selection against heavily damaged/mutated cells.
J2
Professor Jacobson Q2: How many different DNA sequences can code for an average human protein? Why don’t all codes work in practice?
Answer
Because the genetic code is degenerate, most amino acids have multiple synonymous codons (often 2–6). So the number of possible DNA sequences for a protein is approximately the product of the codon-degeneracies for each amino acid position:

# sequences ≈ Π(degeneracy of AAi)

This grows astronomically with protein length: even a few hundred amino acids yields an enormous number of possible coding sequences.

Why not all work in practice: codon bias & tRNA availability, mRNA structure and stability, GC content extremes, repeats, cryptic splice sites, unintended regulatory motifs, problematic secondary structure near the RBS/start codon, and effects on translation speed that can change co-translational folding.
L1
Dr. LeProust Q1: What’s the most commonly used method for oligo synthesis currently?
Answer
The standard commercial approach is solid-phase phosphoramidite chemistry, where nucleoside phosphoramidites are coupled stepwise in repeated synthesis cycles (detritylation → coupling → capping → oxidation).
L2
Dr. LeProust Q2: Why is it difficult to make oligos longer than ~200 nt via direct synthesis?
Answer
Each coupling step is slightly less than 100% efficient. Over hundreds of cycles, yield drops exponentially and truncated products accumulate. In addition, side reactions (e.g., depurination), reagent diffusion limits in the growing strand/support pores, and cumulative damage during repeated chemical steps reduce quality and full-length fraction.
L3
Dr. LeProust Q3: Why can’t you make a 2000 bp gene via direct oligo synthesis?
Answer
A 2000-cycle chemical synthesis would have catastrophic yield loss and extremely high truncation/error accumulation. Instead, long genes are typically produced by assembling shorter oligos (e.g., PCR assembly / Gibson / ligation), then cloning and sequence-verifying the assembled product.
C1
George Church Q1: What are the 10 essential amino acids in all animals, and how does this affect your view of the “Lysine Contingency”?
Answer
The 10 essentials (diet-required): Phenylalanine, Valine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Methionine, Leucine, Isoleucine, Lysine, Histidine, and Arginine (often semi-essential in adults, required for growth in young animals).

Impact on “Lysine Contingency”: animals are already naturally “lysine contingent,” so relying on lysine dependence is a weak fail-safe. Lysine exists throughout food webs, so organisms can often find enough in the wild. Stronger biocontainment shifts to synthetic contingency—engineering dependence on a non-canonical amino acid not found in nature.
C2
George Church Q2: Given AA:NA and NA:NA codes — what code would you suggest for AA:AA interactions?
Answer (practical “AA:AA code” proposal)
For AA:AA interactions, a useful “code” is not a simple 1D alphabet mapping like DNA base pairing, but a combinatorial interface code defined by:

  • Shape complementarity (sterics / geometry of surfaces)
  • Electrostatics (charge-charge patterns)
  • Hydrophobic patterning (buried nonpolar patches)
  • Directional hydrogen bonding networks

In engineered systems, a clean implementation is to build libraries of orthogonal binders (e.g., designed coiled-coil pairs, designed interface proteins) where each pair is “addressed” by a defined interaction motif and minimized cross-talk—functionally serving as an AA:AA interaction code.
